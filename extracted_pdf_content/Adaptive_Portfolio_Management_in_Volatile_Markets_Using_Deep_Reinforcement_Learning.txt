Abstract—Asset prices are highly volatile as the market is
driven by macro-economic events, sentiments and geopolitics,
a lot of time is spent understanding the changing dynamics
to be one step ahead in making investment and trading calls.
Traditionally, these dynamic conditions have caused troubles to
the traditional portfolio management system, which is based
on static models, to allocate suboptimally. Such limitations are
addressed in this research by using Deep Reinforcement Learning
real time portfolio optimization earning (DRL). Specifically, we
explore three DRL algorithms: Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C),
Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG), Proximal Policy
Optimization(PPO). In this research, the DDPG outperforms the
others in terms of its annual return 23.1%, the PPO has the
highest Sharpe ratio 0.998 for risk adjusted returns and A2C
achieves long-term stability with balanced risk.
Index Terms—Deep reinforcement learning, portfolio manage-
ment, machine learning, stocks.
I. I NTRODUCTION
Portfolio management has long relied on models like
Mean-Variance Optimization which aim to balance return
and risk using historical averages [1]. While these traditional
methods can be effective in stable market conditions, they
often fail to adapt when faced with the complexities and rapid
changes of modern financial environments [2]. The limitations
of static models have led to a shift toward more dynamic,
data-driven strategies capable of learning and evolving in real
time. Reinforcement Learning (RL), to be precise Actor-Critic
models, has evolved as a powerful alternative [3]. Unlike
traditional approaches, RL algorithms continuously optimize
asset allocations by interacting with a simulated financial
environment, allowing them to adapt to changing market
dynamics and offering an edge over static techniques [4].
This research explores a system for managing portfolios
using Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), making use
of significant reinforcement learning techniques such as
Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Deep Deterministic
Policy Gradient (DDPG), and Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C)
[5], [6]. These models are designed to make adaptive
investment decisions, dynamically adjusting asset weights to
balance returns and risk in a continuous action space [7].
By leveraging the Actor-Critic architecture, the framework
learns to optimize long-term performance and respond
effectively to volatile market conditions [8]. In this research,
the DRL algorithms are applied to a financial dataset with
major stock index, i.e., the Dow Jones Industrial Average
(DJIA) [9]. The adaptability of DRL models is further
enhanced through a load-on-demand mechanism, which
ensures efficient handling of extensive datasets, reducing
computational overhead [10]. Using important measures like
as the Sharpe ratio, cumulative returns, and Max drawdown,
our study compares the performance of different models
and shows that the DRL-based system performs better than
conventional approaches and provides a flexible solution
for a range of risk profiles and investment objectives. The
results of this study highlight how reinforcement learning
has the potential to revolutionize portfolio management by
offering a strong and flexible framework that can handle the
intricacies of contemporary financial markets. Determining
how to distribute resources across a collection of assets
in order to maximize returns while limiting risk is the
fundamental problem of portfolio management. Traditional
models often fail in their adaptability and rely heavily on
assumptions from historical data. In this study, our objective
is to create an adaptive portfolio management model using
reinforcement learning. The goal is to dynamically adjust
asset weights based on historical market data, which provides
a continuous feedback loop that enables the model to improve
allocation decisions over time. This research brings forward
a comprehensive RL-based approach for dynamic portfolio
management using Actor-Critic models, comparing A2C,
DDPG, and PPO in terms of their strengths, weaknesses,
and performance evaluation indicators including maximum
drawdown, Sharpe ratio and annual return to evaluate their
capacity to dynamically balance risk and return, ultimately
providing insights into the practicality of reinforcement
learning in financial management.
The goal of this paper is to develop and evaluate a rein-
forcement learning (RL) framework for portfolio management,
leveraging advanced algorithms to optimize asset allocations
dynamically. Key objectives include:
• Assessing RL Algorithm Effectiveness: Evaluate A2C,
DDPG, and PPO in portfolio management, analyzing raw
returns and risk-adjusted metrics like the Sharpe ratio.

II. R ELATED WORKS
DRL has emerged as a state-of-the-art tool in the field of
portfolio management, offering unique approaches for improv-
ing investment strategies in increasingly complicated financial
markets. Researchers are utilizing DRL’s capacity to process
vast volumes of financial data and modify policies in real time,
attempting to develop models that balance rewards with risk
under volatile market situations. These new efforts underscore
DRL’s potential to alter portfolio management by allowing
more adaptive, risk-sensitive investment strategies that respond
dynamically to market turbulance.
Liu et al. (2021) [11] used developed DRL trading strategies
with adaptive mechanisms to portfolio optimization across
highly volatile market settings. Their Method implemented
real-time policy manipulation, enabling a Equilibrium between
reward maximization and risk minimization, which showed
adaptability to quick market changes. This study underscores
the benefits of DRL in maintaining consistent portfolio returns
while responding dynamically to market turbulance.
In 2022, Tan et al. proposed a multi-agent DRL framework for
portfolio management [12], focusing on collaborative decision-
making across various assets/stocks. The agents interact within
a environment that was simulated, exchanging insights to
promote diversification and decrease danger. This approach
showed potential in developing a stable and diversified port-
folio by dispersing risk across assets, making the model robust
against severe financial turbulance.
Wang et al. (2023) [13] made yet another improvement in
DRL-based portfolio management by using the PPO algorithm
in multi-asset trading. By managing a wide set asset, PPO was
able to enhance returns while decreasing drawdown risks in
challenging market situations. Their work showed PPO’s ro-
bustness in managing severely volatile trading circumstances,
hence, boosting its applicability in real-world financial con-
texts.
In 2024, Zhang et al. advanced portfolio management through
a dual-network DRL model integrating actor-critic methods
with risk-aversion strategies [14]. This conservative yet ef-
fective approach enabled the model to perform well in un-
stable markets, prioritizing stable returns and safeguarding
against large drawdowns. By combining risk-aware policies
with actor-critic DRL methods, this model offered a more
reliable approach to managing high-risk assets, showcasing
the potential of DRL in robust portfolio management.
While earlier techniques have concentrated on substantial
training for individual asset behaviors, our study diverges by
stressing a generalist strategy across varied portfolios, aiming
for resilience and efficiency under time constraints These
research studies demonstrate a trend toward strengthening
the adaptability and resilience of DRL models for financial
applications.
III. METHODOLOGY
This section discusses the methodology used in this paper.
After selecting the Dow and Jones 30 dataset, the next and
most important step is to prepare it. This step is called
data preprocessing and includes data cleaning, normalization,
and feature scaling. The dataset is analyzed and used to
train deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agents, including
A2C, DDPG, and PPO. The model implemented with these
algorithms interacts with an environment consisting of stock
prices, portfolio holdings, and technical indicators. The agent
selects actions—buy, sell, or hold—based on the observed state
and receives rewards based on profit or loss. The trained model
is then evaluated through backtesting, where performance
metrics such as annual return, Sharpe ratio, and cumulative
return are calculated to optimize the trading strategy.This
process is shown in Figure 1.
A. Dataset Description
The dataset comprises daily historical stock data for the
Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA), encompassing impor-
tant measures like ‘Open,’ ‘High,’ ‘Low,’ ‘Close,’ ‘Adj Close,’
‘V olume,’ and ‘Ticker.’ It comprises corporations spanning
industries such as technology (Apple, Microsoft), finance
(JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs), and consumer goods (Procter
& Gamble, Coca-Cola), along with healthcare, energy, and
industrial enterprises.
The dataset facilitates financial study in time-series analysis,
stock forecasting, and portfolio management through price
swings, adjusted closing values, and volume data. Its sector
variety facilitates cross-industry studies, helping understand
market sentiment and economic trends.

C. Models Used
1) A2C: Among the most prominent RL algorithms is
Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C), which is an improvement over
traditional policy gradient methods. incorporates the advan-
tages of policy gradient and value-based approaches by adding
the advantage function A(s,a) [18]. This function measures the
relative merit of an action compared to a baseline action in
the current state and is defined as:
As, a = Qs,a − Vs (7)
where Q is the action-value function, representing the
expected return for taking action in state s, and V is the
state-value function, representing the expected return from
state s. This separation of value components enhances learning
efficiency by reducing variance in policy updates [19].
2) PPO: Another widely used algorithm is Proximal Policy
Optimization (PPO), which aims to optimize the policy func-
tion while maintaining stability. This is achieved by limiting
the updates to the policy, preventing radical changes that could
destabilize learning [20]. The PPO objective function can be
expressed as:
Lclipθ = Et∼min(rt,θ) [At,clip(rt, 1 − ϵ, 1 + ϵ)] (8)
where At is the estimated advantage and rt(θ) is the probabil-
ity ratio between the new and previous policies. The clipping
operation prevents the policy update from deviating too far
from the current policy, ensuring smooth learning [5].
3) DDPG: The Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient
(DDPG) algorithm employs deep neural networks to approx-
imate both the policy and value functions. It extends DPG to
work in continuous action spaces, where deterministic policies
are preferable. The update rule for the policy network in
DDPG is given by:
ϑ ← ϑ + α∇ϑJϑ (9)
where α is the learning rate. DDPG’s architecture, com-
bining actor and critic networks, enables effective learning in
high-dimensional action spaces [21].

V. C ONCLUSION
In this study, we suggested a DRL framework for portfolio
management, using the strengths of three unique algorithms:
a2c, ddpg, and ppo. By implementing these models, our
framework responds successfully to different market condi-
tions, allowing dynamic portfolio modifications and supporting
various investment aims. To be precise, DDPG displays capa-
bility for maximum returns, matching well with high-growth,
aggressive strategies, while ppo exhibits strong risk-adjusted
returns, offering stability fit for conservative portfolios. Our
results shows the DRL framework’s potential to surpass exist-
ing techniques through real-time, data-driven decision-making.
Our trials shows us that ddpg achieves the maximum yearly
return at 23.1 % but with larger drawdown, whereas ppo gives
the highest Sharpe ratio of 0.998, excelling in risk-adjusted re-
turns a2c demonstrates long-term stability with balanced risk.
However, challenges in interpretability and high computational
requirements remain. Future research could address these by
integrating advanced algorithms, such as Soft Actor-Critic
(SAC) or Recurrent Reinforcement Learning (RRL), to refine
model performance in complex trading scenarios. Additionally,
multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) could enhance
adaptability by simulating the dynamic interactions among
market participants, while expanding data sources to include
macroeconomic and sentiment indicators may further im-
prove predictive accuracy. By applying explainable AI (XAI)
methods, the interpretability of DRL-based models could
be improved, fostering transparency and trust in automated
investment decisions. Through continued refinement and test-
ing in live environments, this framework could significantly
advance automated portfolio management, with implications
for efficiency, robustness, and strategy personalization.
